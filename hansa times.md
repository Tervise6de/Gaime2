# Hansa Times — Historical Reference for Gaime2

> **Purpose.** Fact-based historical reference on the **Hanseatic League ("die
> Hanse") era**, covering roughly **1150–1700** (the League's active life plus
> ~100 years on either side), with the focus on the core period **c. 1250–1550**.
> It exists to feed *real*, verifiable material into Gaime2's systems — economy,
> population, military, diplomacy, tech, buildings, events — so the game's flavour
> and mechanics rest on history, not invention.
>
> **Sourcing rule.** Everything below is drawn from the cited web sources
> (see [Sources](#sources)). Figures are given as the sources give them; where a
> number is approximate it is marked "c." (circa). **Legendary or disputed
> material is explicitly flagged** — most notably Klaus Störtebeker. Nothing in
> this file is a game-design assumption dressed up as history; the game-design
> interpretation is quarantined to the final section,
> [Game-Design Hooks](#12-game-design-hooks-for-gaime2).

## 2. Overall situation: how it happened

The Hanseatic League was **never formally founded** — it has no charter and no
foundation date. It grew out of the self-interest of North German merchants who
needed *security and privileges* while trading across a lawless, politically
fragmented Baltic and North Sea world.

**The pre-conditions.** Before the Hansa, **Visby on Gotland** was the leading
Baltic trade centre. Gotland merchants had already pushed east — establishing the
**Gutagard (Gotenhof)** post at Novgorod around **1080**. The Baltic itself was
ringed by regions producing exactly what the rest of Europe wanted: fish from
Scandinavian waters, grain from Prussia and Poland, timber and naval stores from
the eastern Baltic forests, furs from the Russian interior, amber from the
Prussian coast, and wax from Russian and Polish hives. German merchants sat in
the **central position** between these eastern producers and the western
consuming markets (Flanders, England, the Rhineland) — and that geography is the
whole story of why they came to dominate.

**Lübeck.** A town was built on Lübeck's Old Town island in **1143** under German
law; **Henry the Lion**, Duke of Saxony, captured and rebuilt it in **1158/1159**.
Historians traditionally treat this as the League's origin point. After Henry's
rebuild, Lübeck became the base for Westphalian and Saxon merchants expanding
north and east. The **Artlenburg Privilege (1161)** settled bloody disputes
between the Low German merchants and their Gotland competitors, letting the two
communities cooperate. Lübeck received **imperial freedom in 1226** and became the
"**Queen of the Hanseatic League**" and its de facto capital — "almost all trade
in the Baltic went through" it.

**From merchants to towns.** The decisive institutional shift came with the
**first general Diet (Hansetag) at Lübeck in 1356**, which gave the League
official status and moved it from a "**Hanse of the Merchants**" (individual
traders banding together abroad) to a "**Hanse of the Towns**" (member cities
acting collectively). At its height the network embraced roughly **200 towns**
across central and northern Europe (a commonly cited figure; only a fraction were
consistently active), most clustered around the Baltic.

**The decline** (see §9 for the wars): rising competition from **Dutch and
English** merchants; the strengthening of **national states** (Denmark, Sweden,
England, Muscovy); loss of the eastern hub when **Moscow closed the Novgorod
Peterhof in 1494**; catastrophic overreach in the **Count's Feud (1534–36)**; the
**expulsion from London (1597–98)**; and finally the **Thirty Years' War
(1618–48)**, which shattered trade networks and commercial confidence. The
**last Hansetag met in 1669** with only six cities present and achieved nothing —
the effective end. Lübeck, Hamburg and Bremen kept the "Hanseatic" title into
modern times, but the institution was dead.

## 3. How the League actually worked (organization)

- **No state, no standing army, no permanent navy, no common treasury, no
  written constitution.** The Hansa was a *commercial and defensive network of
  merchant guilds and market towns*, held together by shared interest, shared
  law, and shared privileges — not by sovereignty. This is its single most
  distinctive feature and its ultimate weakness (see §12).
- **The Hansetag (Diet)** was the closest thing to a central organ: an
  irregular assembly of delegates from member towns, first held formally at
  **Lübeck in 1356**. Decisions ("Recesse") depended on towns choosing to comply
  — enforcement was weak and consensus hard to reach.
- **Lübeck** functioned as the leading city and usual host of the Diet.
- **Town leagues within the League.** Regional groupings existed — e.g. the
  **Wendish towns** (Lübeck, Hamburg, Wismar, Lüneburg, Rostock, Stralsund) formed
  the core Baltic bloc and drove both coinage policy and war policy.
- **Shared municipal law.** Many towns were governed under **Lübeck Law**, the
  second most widespread municipal law in medieval Germany after **Magdeburg Law**
  (see §11). Common law + common commercial custom made the network legible and
  self-policing.
- **The ultimate weapon was economic, not military:** the League could
  **blockade or boycott** a hostile ruler or town, cutting it off from Baltic
  trade — and when it *did* fight, it did so by chartering town fleets ad hoc
  (as through the **Confederation of Cologne, 1367**), not by maintaining a navy.

## 4. The Kontore (foreign trading posts)

The four principal **Kontore** were the cornerstones of Hanseatic trade:
**Novgorod, London, Bruges, and Bergen**. A Kontor was an organized settlement
run as a **private corporation** — with its own **treasury, court, statutes, and
seal**, and its own legal jurisdiction. They were **extraterritorial** enclaves
enjoying considerable legal autonomy, run by **aldermen elected annually**. Their
functions: security for merchants abroad, quality-control of goods (inspected on
site, which raised trade efficiency), securing and defending privileges, and
diplomacy with local rulers.

| Kontor | Location | Trade focus | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Peterhof** | **Novgorod** (Russia) | Furs, wax, honey, timber ↔ cloth, salt, metal goods | Named after its **church of St. Peter**. Gateway to the Russian interior. Peaked at **100+ German merchants c. 1400**. **Closed by Ivan III of Moscow in 1494**, merchants deported. |
| **Steelyard** (Stalhof) | **London** | English wool & cloth | West of London Bridge near Upper Thames Street (later the site of Cannon Street station). A **walled community** with warehouses, weigh-house, church, offices and homes. Had both a Hanseatic **and** an English alderman. Expelled by **Elizabeth I in 1597**; closed/sequestered **1598**; briefly returned under James I (1606) but never recovered. |
| **Bruges Kontor** | **Bruges** (Flanders) | Western hub — cloth, luxury goods, exchange | The great western clearing-house where merchants "from all over the known world" met. Unusual in that it **acquired its own buildings only from the 15th century** (before that it operated through the town itself). |
| **Bryggen** (Tyske Bryggen) | **Bergen** (Norway) | **Stockfish** (dried cod) | Formalized **1343** under Norwegian royal concession. Housed **up to ~2,000 traders** at its height. The **only Kontor whose buildings survive** — colorful gabled **wooden** houses along Bergen harbour; ~60 remain today as a **UNESCO World Heritage** site despite repeated fires. Operated until **1754**. |

## 5. Trade goods & the trade network (resources)

**The single commercial system.** The North Sea and Baltic together formed one
great commercial system. Goods flowed **westward** (pelts, wax, grain, flax,
timber, beer, fish) and were exchanged for goods flowing **eastward** (textiles,
salt, wine, metal products, spices, luxury goods).

**Key commodities:**

- **Salt** — the "**white gold**." The most jealously guarded commodity, mined
  chiefly at **Lüneburg**. In a world without refrigeration it was the only
  reliable way to preserve food, above all fish. The whole herring economy
  depended on it.
- **Herring & dried fish** — **salted herring** distributed across Europe;
  **stockfish** (dried cod) from Norway via Bergen. The Treaty of Stralsund
  (1370) effectively gave the Hansa a **monopoly on the Baltic fish trade**.
- **Grain** — from Prussia and Poland (via Danzig), feeding the west.
- **Timber & naval stores** — wood, plus **ash, pitch, tar** from the eastern
  Baltic forests.
- **Furs & pelts** — from the Russian interior via Novgorod.
- **Wax** — from Russian and Polish hives (for candles, seals).
- **Amber** — from the Prussian coast.
- **Wool & cloth** — English wool and woollen/linen textiles; the western trade's
  backbone (and the flashpoint of the English conflicts).
- **Flax & hemp** — for cloth and cordage.
- **Beer** — a major manufactured export. **Wendish towns (Lübeck, Hamburg,
  Wismar, Rostock)** built **export breweries** for **hopped beer**; Hanseatic
  beer was the most valued.
- **Wine, metal goods, spices** — imported luxuries moving east.

**Production chains worth modelling** (the game-relevant part):

```
Lüneburg salt  +  Scandinavian herring  → salted herring → sold Europe-wide
Norwegian cod  → (Bergen) stockfish     → sold Europe-wide
grain (Prussia/Poland) → Danzig → western markets
Russian forest/hives → (Novgorod) furs, wax → western luxury markets
English wool → cloth → (Bruges/Steelyard) distributed
grain + hops → hopped beer (Wendish breweries) → export
```

## 6. Buildings & architecture

The signature style is **Brick Gothic (Backsteingotik)** — Gothic architecture
executed in fired brick (the Baltic plain has little building stone). It defines
the look of the Hansa world and is ideal reference for a low-art game's building
set.

**Real, dated examples:**

- **St. Mary's Church (Marienkirche), Lübeck** — begun **13th century**; its
  brick vaults are the **highest of any brick church in the world** and it
  inspired **70+ "daughter churches"** across the Baltic. The template building of
  the whole style.
- **Holstentor, Lübeck** — the iconic twin-towered brick city gate. Sources give
  completion **c. 1464**, with the famous form associated with **1478**. Its
  towers visibly **lean** from centuries of foundation settlement.
- **Lübeck Rathaus (Town Hall)** — built from the **late 13th century**; fuses
  Gothic and later Renaissance elements; distinctive wind-hole arcades and shields
  on the façade.
- **Salzspeicher (Salt Warehouses), Lübeck** — six stepped-gable brick
  warehouses (**16th–18th c.**) beside the Holstentor, built to store the
  Lüneburg salt that underwrote the city's wealth.
- **Bryggen, Bergen** — the surviving **wooden** Kontor warehouses (gabled houses
  along the harbour); ~60 remain (UNESCO). Note the contrast: **Norway's Kontor
  was timber**, not brick.
- **London Steelyard** — a walled compound: warehouses, weigh-house, church,
  offices, dwellings (no longer standing; site is Cannon Street).
- **Bremen Town Hall & Roland** — the Gothic **Rathaus** (early 15th c., after
  Bremen joined the Hansa) and the **Bremen Roland (1404)**, a **5.5 m stone
  statue** symbolizing the city's freedom, market rights (Roland's gloves) and
  independent judicial authority — under merchants and guilds, not church or
  feudal lord. **Roland statues** were erected in market squares across central
  Europe as emblems of market rights and civic freedom. (UNESCO World Heritage.)

**Building types to draw on** (all attested for the era/region):

- Brick Gothic **church** (Marienkirche type) — civic prestige / prosperity marker.
- **Rathaus (town hall)** — seat of the merchant-council government.
- **Speicher / Salzspeicher (warehouse / salt store)** — storage & trade capacity.
- **Kontor compound** — fortified/walled foreign trading enclave.
- **City gate & walls** (Holstentor type) — fortification.
- **Roland statue** — civic freedom / legal autonomy monument.
- **Export brewery** — manufacturing (hopped beer).
- **Merchant's house** with ground-floor hall and gabled façade.
- **Canal / waterway** (Stecknitz, 1398) — logistics infrastructure.

## 7. Famous people (real, dated, why they matter)

- **Henry the Lion (1129/30–1195)** — Duke of Saxony (from 1142) and Bavaria (from
  1156), of the **Guelph (Welf)** dynasty. Captured and rebuilt **Lübeck
  (1158/59)** and issued the **Artlenburg Privilege (1161)**, elevating Lübeck
  (and Munich) to commercial prominence. Ruled a vast territory from the North/
  Baltic coasts to the Alps until his fall in **1180** (outlawed after clashing
  with Emperor **Frederick I Barbarossa**). *Why he matters:* the founding
  patron of the city that became the Hansa's capital.

- **Valdemar IV "Atterdag" of Denmark (c. 1320–1375)** — King **1340–1375**.
  **Reunified Denmark** after it had collapsed into bankruptcy and mortgaged
  territory ("Atterdag" ≈ "day again"). In **1361 conquered Scania, Öland, and
  Gotland (with Visby)** — the act that triggered the great Danish–Hanseatic War.
  His daughter **Margaret's** marriage set up the union of Denmark and Norway.
  *Why he matters:* the League's chief royal antagonist; the villain of the
  1361–70 war.

- **Margaret I of Denmark (1353–1412)** — daughter of Valdemar IV; Queen of
  Denmark, Norway and Sweden and **founder of the Kalmar Union (1397)**, which
  united the three Scandinavian crowns under her great-nephew **Erik** (crowned
  17 June 1397) while she held real power until her death in **1412**. *Why she
  matters:* she built the Scandinavian super-state that became the Hansa's
  strategic counterweight in the Baltic.

- **Klaus Störtebeker (c. 1360 – 1400 or 1401)** — German pirate, reputed leader
  of the **Victual Brothers (Vitalienbrüder)**, privateers originally hired in the
  Sweden–Denmark war who then preyed on merchant shipping in the Baltic and North
  Seas. They called themselves **"Likedeelers" ("equal sharers")**. Reputedly
  **beheaded with ~70 of his crew on Hamburg's Grasbrook on 20 October 1401**.
  ⚠️ **Legend vs. fact:** Störtebeker is a **folk-hero (Robin Hood-like)** figure
  and much is uncorroborated. Modern research questions the traditional date — a
  surviving Hamburg **grave-digging bill for 30 Victual Brothers points to 1400**,
  not 1401. The famous tale that he walked past 11 of his men headless to save
  them is a **chronicle legend, not corroborated**. *Why he matters:* the era's
  emblem of piracy and the merchant-vs-pirate conflict — but treat the biography
  as legend.

- **Jürgen Wullenwever (c. 1492–1537)** — **Burgomaster of Lübeck 1533–1535**, a
  radical reformist mayor. Pushed Lübeck into the **Count's Feud (1534–36)** to
  restore Hanseatic dominance of the Baltic and shut the **Dutch** out of the
  Danish Sound. The gamble failed; he was ejected, later **tortured and executed
  at Wolfenbüttel (1537)**. *Why he matters:* his overreach broke Lübeck's power
  and marks the political end of Hanseatic dominance.

- **Ulrich von Jungingen (c. 1360–1410)** — Grand Master of the **Teutonic
  Order**, killed leading the Order to catastrophic defeat at **Grunwald /
  Tannenberg (1410)**. *Why he matters:* Grunwald broke the Order that ruled
  Prussia and its Hanseatic cities (Danzig, Thorn, Elbing), reshaping the eastern
  Baltic.

- **Casimir IV Jagiellon (1427–1492)** — King of Poland; the Prussian
  Confederation appealed to him in **1454**, leading to the **Thirteen Years' War**
  and the transfer of **Danzig, Thorn, Elbing** to Poland (Second Peace of Thorn,
  1466). *Why he matters:* he pulled the richest Prussian Hansa towns out of the
  Teutonic Order and into Poland.

- **Edward IV of England (1442–1483)** — conceded almost all Hanseatic demands in
  the **Treaty of Utrecht (1474)** after losing the Anglo-Hanseatic War, keeping
  the Steelyard's privileges intact for another century.

- **Elizabeth I of England (1533–1603)** — **expelled the League from London in
  1597**; the Steelyard was closed in **1598**. *Why she matters:* symbolic end of
  Hanseatic power in England, in favour of English (Merchant Adventurers) trade.

## 8. Ships

**The cog (Kogge)** — the workhorse and symbol of Hanseatic trade.

- **Construction:** **clinker-built** (overlapping oak planks), single mast,
  single **square sail**, **straight keel**, high sides, raking stemposts, and a
  **fixed stern (pintle-and-gudgeon) rudder** — a robust, capacious cargo hull.
  Widespread from the **12th century**.
- **Size / capacity:** typical seagoing cogs were **c. 15–25 m long, 5–8 m wide,
  and of 30–200 tons burthen**. Used above all by the Hanseatic League for NW
  European trade.
- **The Bremen Cog (c. 1380)** — the **best-preserved medieval merchant ship in
  the world**, found in the Weser in **1962**, dendro-dated to **c. 1380**.
  **23.27 m long, 7.62 m wide, 7.02 m high** to the top of the capstan; round
  hull, clinker oak planking, single square sail, and a **high stern castle** for
  defence and cargo handling. It is *the* reference object for a Hansa ship.

**The hulk / holk (hulc)** — a large cargo carrier that gradually **succeeded the
cog** as ships grew in the 15th century (bigger burden, different hull form).

**The transition to larger, ocean-capable ships** (the cutting edge of the era,
mostly beyond the Hansa's own use but part of the world it lived in):

- **Carrack (nau/não)** — a **14th–17th c.** ship, usually **three-masted**
  (square-rigged fore and main, **lateen** mizzen), **carvel-built** (planks
  edge-to-edge, flush). Its **high sides** made it hard to board and gave room to
  **mount cannon** — carrack sterns "bristled with cannons." The ship of the Age
  of Exploration and of naval gunnery.
- **Caravel** — a **mid-15th c. Portuguese** design; smaller, low draught,
  lateen (later mixed) sails, highly manoeuvrable. The exploration scout.
- **Fluyt** — a **late-16th c. Dutch** purpose-built merchantman: box-like hull,
  huge hold, cheap to run, minimal armament. It **out-competed the older Hanseatic
  carriers on cost** — a ship that helped end Hansa commercial dominance.

**Two decisive shipbuilding transitions to model:**

1. **Clinker → carvel.** Northern clinker construction (cog) gives way to
   Mediterranean-derived **carvel** (carrack), enabling bigger, gun-carrying hulls.
2. **One mast → three masts + guns.** The single-masted square-sail cog gives way
   to the **three-masted, mixed-rig, cannon-armed** carrack/galleon — i.e. the
   birth of the sailing warship and of naval artillery.

## 9. Wars & conflicts

### Danish–Hanseatic War (1361–1370) and the Peace of Stralsund

- **Trigger (1361):** King **Valdemar IV** conquered **Scania, Öland, and
  Gotland (with the major Hansa town Visby)**.
- **1362:** a Hanseatic counterstrike was **repelled by the Danish fleet at
  Helsingborg**, forcing the League into the unfavourable **Treaty of
  Vordingborg**, stripping privileges.
- **1367:** refusing to accept defeat, the towns formed the **Confederation of
  Cologne**, raised a fleet, and renewed their Swedish alliances — the moment a
  loose trade league acted as a war coalition.
- **Peace of Stralsund (24 May 1370):** the League **won**. Visby's freedom was
  restored; Denmark guaranteed **free Hanseatic trade throughout the Baltic**,
  waived taxes on German merchants in Scania, and (per some sources) gave the
  Hansa influence over the Danish succession and control of key Scania castles.
  This gave the League a **monopoly on the Baltic fish trade** and marks the
  **peak of Hanseatic power**.

### Piracy: the Victual Brothers (Vitalienbrüder), c. 1390s–1400s

Privateers hired in the Sweden–Denmark conflict who turned to open piracy across
the Baltic and North Sea; associated with **Klaus Störtebeker** (see §7 — largely
legendary biography). Their suppression (executions at Hamburg, **1400/1401**) is
the era's set-piece merchant-vs-pirate conflict.

### The Teutonic Order and the Prussian Hansa towns

- **Battle of Grunwald / Tannenberg (15 July 1410):** **Poland-Lithuania**
  (Władysław II Jagiełło & Vytautas) **decisively defeated the Teutonic Order**
  (Grand Master **Ulrich von Jungingen**, killed). The Order never recovered its
  dominance; war reparations wrecked its finances.
- **Thirteen Years' War / War of the Cities (1454–1466):** the **Prussian
  Confederation** of towns rose against the Order and appealed to **Poland**.
  **Danzig, Thorn, and Elbing** became part of **Royal Prussia under Poland**
  (Second Peace of Thorn, 1466). ⚠️ **Notable irony:** although the Hansa
  *sympathized* with the Prussian towns, **the League backed the Teutonic Order**
  because the Order had granted it extra privileges — a good example of
  self-interest overriding solidarity.
- **Danzig's rise:** full Hansa member from **1361**; by **1377** it had passed
  Elbing and Thorn to become **Prussia's most prosperous city**, and by the early
  15th century it **rivalled Lübeck** for leadership of the League itself.

### Anglo-Hanseatic conflicts

- **Long-running friction** over the English wool/cloth trade: English merchants
  (later the **Merchant Adventurers**) resented Hansa privileges; **Henry VI
  revoked Hansa privileges in 1447**.
- **Anglo-Hanseatic War (1469–1474):** led by **Danzig and Lübeck**. Triggered
  when **Danzig privateers seized English ships in the Sound (1468)** and
  **Edward IV foreclosed and seized the London Steelyard (1469)**. Fought as
  **commerce raiding** in the North Sea and Channel; the Danzig man-of-war
  **_Peter von Danzig_** was a famed raider.
- **Treaty of Utrecht (1474):** Edward IV **conceded almost everything** — Hansa
  privileges restored, **ownership of the Steelyard** confirmed, plus bases at
  **Boston and Lynn** and guaranteed access to **Hull, Lynn, Boston**. A late
  Hanseatic diplomatic victory — the Merchant Adventurers were shut out of the
  Baltic for a generation.
- **Endgame:** **Elizabeth I expelled the League from London (1597)**; the
  Steelyard closed **1598**.

### The eastern collapse: Novgorod, 1494

**Ivan III of Moscow closed the Novgorod Peterhof in 1494** and deported its
German merchants to Moscow, to break Hanseatic influence over Russian trade. The
League lost its gateway to Russian furs and wax — a structural blow to the eastern
network.

### The Count's Feud (Grevens Fejde, 1534–1536)

A **Danish war of succession** entangled with the **Reformation**, named for the
Protestant count **Christopher of Oldenburg** (championing the deposed Catholic
king **Christian II**). **Lübeck under Jürgen Wullenwever** financed and navally
backed Christopher, aiming to restore Baltic dominance and **exclude the Dutch
from the Danish straits**. It **failed**: **Christian III** won, Copenhagen
surrendered after a year's siege (29 July 1536), Wullenwever fell and was executed
(1537). *Result:* the Hansa cities **lost their influence in the Baltic**; Lübeck
went into permanent decline; **Hamburg** (which traded freely with the Dutch)
rose in its place; and Christian III turned **Norway into a Danish puppet
kingdom (1537)**, ending its Kalmar-era equality.

### The final blow: Dutch/English competition and the Thirty Years' War

By the 16th century the League was losing to **Dutch and English** merchants (the
**fluyt** undercut Hanseatic shipping on cost) and to strengthening **national
states**. The **Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)** shattered trade networks and
commercial confidence even in unbesieged cities. The **1669 Hansetag** (six cities)
was the quiet end.

## 10. Military equipment — region and wider world

The Hansa itself was not a military power in the field — it fought at sea (armed
cogs, hired town fleets) and behind **brick city walls and gates**. But it lived
through one of history's great military revolutions, and a strategy game set here
should reflect the transitions below.

### Land warfare in Europe (the transitions)

- **Armour: mail → plate.** From the **early 14th century** armourers added plate
  pieces to mail specifically to counter **longbow and crossbow** arrows;
  **transitional armour** (mail + plates over vital areas) culminated in **full
  plate by the 15th century**, peaking in craftsmanship in the late 15th/early
  16th.
- **Missile weapons.** **Crossbows** grew more powerful — 14th-century military
  crossbows used **windlasses** and **cranequins** (rack-and-pinion) to span bows
  strong enough to punch plate. The **English longbow** was the era's other great
  missile weapon.
- **The infantry revolution & pike.** Massed **pike** infantry re-tilted the
  field against cavalry: the **Swiss** perfected pike formations through
  **Morgarten (1315), Laupen (1339), Sempach (1386)** — "nearly invincible against
  cavalry when in formation." The long pike came into mercenary use in Flanders in
  the **mid-15th century**.
- **Gunpowder small arms.** First **hand cannons** appeared with infantry by
  **c. 1415**; the **arquebus** with a **matchlock** appeared in the **later 15th
  century** (matchlock mechanism c. **before 1475**; a crossbow-style **shoulder
  stock** added c. **1470**).
- **Pike-and-shot.** The pike block + gunpowder shot combination became the
  dominant infantry system into the 16th–17th centuries — the tactical world of
  the League's late wars.
- **Artillery & fortification.** Big **bombards** could smash medieval walls
  (dramatized at Constantinople, below). The answer was the **bastion fort /
  trace italienne**, which appeared in **mid-15th-century Italy**: low, thick,
  angled **bastions** eliminating "dead zones," letting flanking fire sweep the
  curtain, making fortresses **assault-proof** and turning sieges into
  months-to-years-long blockades. This is the transition **from tall medieval
  curtain walls (Holstentor era) to gunpowder-age star forts**.

### Naval warfare

Guns went to sea on the new **carvel-built, high-sided carracks**, whose sterns
"bristled with cannons" — the birth of the broadside sailing warship. Hanseatic
naval war, by contrast, was still mostly **armed merchant cogs and hired town
fleets** engaged in **commerce raiding** (as in the Anglo-Hanseatic War's
_Peter von Danzig_).

### The wider world, same window (for contrast)

- **Ottoman Empire — Fall of Constantinople (1453).** Mehmed II's army
  (**c. 50,000–80,000**) included elite **Janissaries** (a corps of forcibly
  converted Christian boys) who were **beginning to carry personal firearms**. Its
  decisive feature was a **siege artillery train of ~70 cannons**, including
  giant **bombards** cast by the engineer **Orban** — the largest ("Basilica")
  reportedly **~27 ft long, firing ~600-lb stone balls**, pounding the
  **Theodosian Walls**. (Sources caution the guns aided but did not alone decide
  the siege — numbers mattered most.)
- **Ming China (1368–1644).** A **wide gunpowder arsenal** — firearms, cannon,
  bombs, rockets, incendiaries — managed by the elite **Shenjiying ("Divine
  Machine Division")**. The **proportion of firearm-equipped troops was higher in
  Ming armies than in Europe from the 1300s to mid-1500s.** But innovation slowed:
  internal peace and a centralized bureaucracy meant Chinese guns were used to
  **maintain the status quo**, and the cutting edge of gun development **shifted to
  Europe**, whose guns grew larger and more efficient.
- **Timurid / Mughal & Indian subcontinent.** The "**gunpowder empires**"
  (Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal) framework applies to the wider Islamic world; firearms
  and artillery spread through the subcontinent across **c. 1230–1526** into the
  Mughal era. (Sources returned less specific detail on Timur's own artillery.)

**Design takeaway for contrast:** in this window, **Europe transitions from
knight-and-castle to pike-shot-and-star-fort**, at sea from **clinker cog to
gun-armed carrack**; **the Ottomans lead in siege artillery**; **Ming China
fields the most gun-heavy infantry but stops innovating**. That's a rich basis for
differentiated factions/tech in a game.

## 11. Economy & society (currency, law, guilds, plague, population)

### Currency & coinage

- The **Wendish Coinage Union (Wendischer Münzverein)** — a **treaty-bound**
  monetary union of North German Hansa towns, **from 1379 into the 16th century**,
  to standardize coinage. Core members: **Lübeck, Hamburg, Wismar, Lüneburg**
  (joined at times by **Rostock, Stralsund, Hanover**).
- The base unit was the **Lübeck Mark (Lübische Mark)**. The union's silver coin
  equivalent to the mark contained **c. 18 g of fine silver** and bore the arms of
  the four member towns.
- The **Witten** (worth **4 Pfennigs**) was minted from **1330** (starting in
  Lübeck, then Hamburg, Wismar, Lüneburg, Rostock, Stralsund) — a **common
  appearance, weight, and fineness** across towns under the coinage treaty.
- The system was **silver-standard** and regulated northern German currency until
  **c. 1569**, influencing all of Scandinavia. *A rare medieval example of a
  cross-city monetary union — great model for a "trade league" mechanic.*

### Town law

- **Lübeck Law** — the **second most widespread municipal law** in medieval/early
  modern Germany (after Magdeburg Law). It provided **municipal self-government**
  (legislative, judicial, executive autonomy) while still acknowledging a lord —
  the legal DNA of Hanseatic towns and a mechanism of network cohesion.
- **Magdeburg Law (Magdeburg Rights)** — town privileges first developed under
  **Otto I**, regulating internal urban autonomy; based on Roman municipal and
  merchant traditions; spread across central and eastern Europe.
- **Guilds** — the Hansa itself was a **network of merchant guilds and market
  towns**; guilds formed both in home towns and destination ports and cooperated
  into the wider network. Civic power lay with **merchant councils and guilds**,
  not church or feudal lords (see the Bremen Roland as its emblem).

### Staple rights & privileges

Kontore existed to **win, hold, and defend commercial privileges** from foreign
rulers; **staple rights** and monopolies (e.g. the Baltic fish monopoly won at
Stralsund in 1370) were the core economic prizes — and the recurring cause of war
when rulers tried to revoke them (England 1447, the whole Anglo-Hanseatic War).

### The Black Death (reached the region 1350)

Plague reached northern Germany / the Hansa ports in **early summer 1350**
(arriving by **maritime trade routes** — the same network that made the towns
rich carried the disease). Documented local impact:

- **Hamburg:** **>6,000 dead in 1350 — roughly half the population.** Council and
  trades gutted: **12 of 34 bakers, 18 of 40 butchers, 27 of 50 civil servants,
  16 of 21 council members** died.
- **Bremen:** the council listed **6,966** named plague dead; with ~1,000
  unknown, against a population of **c. 12,000–15,000**, **more than half** died.
- **Lübeck, Wismar, Reval, Lüneburg:** **30%+ death rates among city-council
  members** alone.
- Summary figure in the sources: **at least 60% of the population of Hamburg and
  Bremen perished.**

*(These are the well-documented regional figures; broader Hansa-wide population
series are patchy — treat town totals like Bremen's ~12–15k as order-of-magnitude.)*

## 12. What was planned or emerging but never fully materialized

Grounded in the sourced history — these are real "roads not taken," useful as
game tension or alternate-history content:

- **A Hanseatic *state*.** The League **never became a polity** — no
  constitution, no sovereign, no capital-as-government, no citizenship. It
  remained a network of self-interested towns. Repeated coordination failure (the
  Hansetag could rarely enforce its own Recesse) meant the "United Provinces of the
  Baltic" that its economic weight could have supported **never formed**. Its
  disunity is explicitly cited as a cause of decline.
- **A permanent Hanseatic navy / standing army.** The League fought by
  **chartering town fleets ad hoc** (Confederation of Cologne, 1367). A permanent
  Baltic navy was never institutionalized — which is exactly why a *single*
  strong king (Denmark) or two commercial rivals (Dutch, English) could eventually
  overpower it piecemeal.
- **Wullenwever's Baltic restoration (1534–36).** Lübeck's bid to **forcibly
  restore Hanseatic dominance and shut the Dutch out of the Sound** was a
  deliberate plan to reverse decline — and its total failure sealed that decline
  instead. A textbook "gamble to reclaim hegemony" that backfired.
- **Reviving the League (1669).** The **last Hansetag considered proposals to
  revive** the institution; nothing came of them. The revival was planned and
  never materialized.
- **What *did* materialize** (so you don't miscode it as vapor): the **Stecknitz
  Canal (1391–98)** — Europe's **first summit-level canal**, a genuinely
  ambitious, *completed* infrastructure project linking North Sea and Baltic
  drainage to move Lüneburg salt. Real, and a great "great-works" reference.

## 13. Game-Design Hooks for Gaime2

> This section is the **only** place where the history is turned into design
> suggestions. It maps the sourced facts above onto Gaime2's existing systems
> (`docs/game-design.md`). Numbers here are *design suggestions for tuning*, not
> historical claims.

### Resources (maps to §3.2 Economy — 4 core + strategic)

The Hansa economy suggests a clean, historically-grounded resource set:

| Gaime2 slot | Hansa mapping |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Gold** | Silver mark / Witten; trade income, tariffs, staple dues |
| **Food** | Grain (Prussia/Poland) + **herring/stockfish** (needs salt) |
| **Materials** | Timber & naval stores (pitch, tar), brick/stone |
| **Knowledge** | Town law/charters, navigation, guild craft |
| **Strategic #1: Salt** | "White gold" (Lüneburg) — **required to preserve fish**; gate the food/trade chain behind it |
| **Strategic #2: Furs/Wax or Amber** | Eastern luxuries via Novgorod — high-value export, tied to specific (Russian-facing) territory |

**Production chain idea (real):** `Salt + Herring → Salted Herring (tradeable food/gold)`;
`Cod → (Bergen) Stockfish`; `Grain + Hops → Hopped Beer (export good)`. This gives
the "every coin denied elsewhere" tension your design wants, with historical skin.

### Buildings (maps to §3.3 region production slots)

Drop-in building list, all historically attested:
**Brick-Gothic Church** (prestige / unrest-reduction, "Great Works" victory
piece), **Rathaus** (governance / tax efficiency), **Speicher / Salzspeicher**
(storage & trade capacity), **Kontor** (foreign-trade node / diplomacy reach),
**City Gate & Walls** (Holstentor → fortification level), **Roland Statue** (civic
freedom / stability monument), **Export Brewery** (manufacturing), **Canal**
(Stecknitz → logistics / adjacency-trade bonus).

### Ships / units (maps to §3.4 Military — the "naval layer" you cut for v1, or a
tech-era flavor of land units)

Even with naval combat cut from v1, ships are perfect **tech-tier flavor** or a
future layer: **Cog** (baseline trade/transport, clinker), **Hulk** (larger cargo),
**Carrack** (carvel, three-masted, **can carry cannon** — the military upgrade),
**Fluyt** (cheap high-capacity trader — an *economic* rival unit that undercuts
you). Two clean **tech transitions to gate**: *clinker→carvel* and *one-mast→
three-mast+guns*.

### Land units & the counter loop (maps to §3.4)

The real military transition slots straight into a rock-paper-scissors + tech
progression: **Militia/Spear** → **Men-at-arms (mail→plate)** → **Crossbow/Longbow**
(cranequin crossbow counters plate) → **Pike block** (anti-cavalry, Swiss-style) →
**Arquebus/Hand-gunner** (gunpowder) → **Pike-and-shot**. Sieges evolve from
**bombard vs. curtain wall** to **star fort (trace italienne)** as a late defensive
tech — a natural "fortification level" upgrade path.

### Factions / AI archetypes (maps to §5 personality archetypes)

Historically-real factions map cleanly onto your Warlord/Merchant/Builder/
Opportunist archetypes:
- **Lübeck / the Hansa towns** — *Merchant* (trade, boycotts, ad-hoc coalitions).
- **Denmark (Valdemar IV / Kalmar Union / Margaret I)** — *Warlord/Opportunist*
  royal power that taxes the Sound and seizes islands.
- **Teutonic Order** — *Warlord/Builder* crusader-state that grants privileges.
- **Muscovy (Ivan III)** — *Opportunist* that can **close your eastern Kontor**.
- **Dutch & English (Merchant Adventurers)** — late-game *Merchant* rivals whose
  **cheaper ships (fluyt)** erode your trade edge — a built-in "external clock."

### Events (maps to §6 bounded random events)

Real, single-beat events ready to use: **Plague (1350)** — halves a region's pop,
guts its council/production; **Kontor seizure** (London 1469 / Novgorod 1494 /
Bergen drift) — lose a foreign-trade node; **Pirate raid (Victual Brothers)** —
lose a trade convoy unless escorted; **Herring monopoly won (Stralsund 1370)** —
trade windfall; **Coinage debasement / union** — currency event; **Great fire at
the wooden Kontor (Bryggen)** — infrastructure loss; **Overreach war
(Wullenwever)** — a "reclaim hegemony" gamble with big downside.

### Systemic themes worth stealing

- **Economic power without a state** — the Hansa's whole existence is your
  "diplomacy/trade to avoid multi-front war" pillar made literal. Boycott/blockade
  as a **non-military coercion tool** is very Hanseatic and underused in 4X.
- **Disunity as the anti-snowball** — the League's failure to coordinate is a
  historical version of your unrest/overexpansion brake: leagues that grow past
  their ability to agree fall apart.
- **The trade network *is* the vulnerability** — the same routes that bring wealth
  bring plague (1350) and expose you to a single hostile king closing a strait or
  a Kontor. Reward the network, but make it a real risk surface.
